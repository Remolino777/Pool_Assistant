1. Core Water Chemistry Parameters & Ideal Ranges
Each parameter is presented with its role, ideal range, and the specific impact on swimmers, surfaces, and equipment.
1.1 Free Chlorine (FC) û Sanitizer
Roleáû Kills bacteria, viruses, algae.
Ideal Rangeáû Without CYA: 1û3 ppm. With CYA 30û50 ppm: 3û5 ppm (minimum FC = 7.5% of CYA, e.g., CYA 40 ? FC ? 3 ppm).
Swimmer Impactáû Too low = unsafe water (risk of illness). Too high (>10 ppm) = skin/eye irritation, strong odor.
Surface Impactáû Very high FC (prolonged shock levels) can fade vinyl liners; low FC allows algae growth ? staining.
Equipment Impactáû Adequate FC prevents chloramines that corrode metal. High FC alone is not corrosive to equipment at normal pH.
Equipment' specific noteáû Salt water chlorine generators (SWGs) maintain FC continuously; still test weekly. Ozone/UV systems reduce FC demand but do not replace a residual.
1.2 Combined Chlorine (CC) û Chloramines
Roleáû Formed when FC combines with ammonia / organics. Indicates oxidation demand.
Ideal Rangeáû 0 ppm (? 0.2 ppm acceptable).
Swimmer Impactáû Causes eye burn, skin irritation, and the ôchlorine smellö.
Surface Impactáû Biofilm promoter; can contribute to algae and surface staining.
Equipment Impactáû Chloramines aggressively attack heat exchangers, pump seals, and stainless steel.
Actionáû Shock to breakpoint (10Î CC reading) if >0.2 ppm (see 3.2).
1.3 pH û Acidity / Alkalinity
Roleáû Determines chlorine efficacy and water balance.
Ideal Rangeáû 7.2û7.8 (optimal 7.4û7.6; salt pools target 7.2û7.6 to reduce scaling).
Swimmer Impactáû <7.0: stings eyes, dries skin. >8.0: chlorine becomes 50%+ less effective, irritation from chloramines.
Surface Impactáû Low pH etches plaster, grout, stone, and corrodes metal fittings. High pH causes calcium carbonate scale on tile, liner, light rings.
Equipment Impactáû Low pH attacks pumps, heaters, ladders (metal corrosion). High pH leads to scale inside pipes, heater elements, salt cells.
Equipment' specific noteáû Gas heaters are highly sensitive to low pH; scale from high pH reduces heat exchanger efficiency and can cause cracking.
1.4 Total Alkalinity (TA) û pH Buffer
Roleáû Bicarbonate buffer that stabilizes pH.
Ideal Rangeáû Plaster: 80û120 ppm; vinyl/fiberglass: 80û100 ppm; SWG pools (scaling risk): 60û80 ppm.
Swimmer Impactáû Low TA ? pH bounce ? eye/skin discomfort. High TA indirectly keeps pH high ? chlorine inefficiency.
Surface Impactáû Low TA = aggressive water (etching plaster). High TA = scale formation when pH rises.
Equipment Impactáû pH instability (low TA) leads to metal corrosion; high TA + high pH = scale on heater cores and salt cells.
Equipment' specific noteáû SWG cells raise pH continuously, so keeping TA lower (6080) helps resist scaling. Cartridge filters clog faster with scale particles.
1.5 Calcium Hardness (CH) û Mineral Balance
Roleáû Prevents water from being too ôhungryö (corrosive) or too ôfullö (scale forming).
Ideal Rangeáû Plaster/pebble: 200û400 ppm; vinyl: 150û250 ppm; fiberglass: 200û300 ppm.
Swimmer Impactáû Not a direct irritant, but scale particles can abrade skin.
Surface Impactáû Low CH: water leaches calcium from plaster/grout ? pitting, roughness. High CH: white scale on waterline and surfaces.
Equipment Impactáû Scale deposits on pump impellers, heater elements, salt cell blades ? reduced flow, overheating, cell failure.
Equipment' specific noteáû Electric heat pumps are particularly susceptible to scale because of high heat exchange surfaces; maintain CH at lower end of range if water is hard.
1.6 Cyanuric Acid (CYA) û Stabilizer
Roleáû Shields chlorine from UV degradation (sunlight).
Ideal Rangeáû Outdoor: 30û50 ppm; indoor: 0û20 ppm. Never >100 ppm; SWG pools may go up to 80 ppm.
Swimmer Impactáû Too low: chlorine burns off ? unsafe water. Too high (>100 ppm): ôchlorine lockö ? algae and illness risk.
Surface Impactáû Indirect û locked chlorine leads to algae, staining surfaces.
Equipment Impactáû High CYA forces much higher FC targets, increasing chemical cost and stressing feeders.
Equipment' specific noteáû Liquid chlorine dosing systems / bleach pumps must be calibrated to the higher FC demand if CYA is high.
1.7 Salt (Sodium Chloride) for SWG Pools
Roleáû Electrolyte for chlorine generation.
Ideal Rangeáû 2700û4500 ppm; typical target 3200 ppm (always per manufacturer).
Swimmer Impactáû Low salt: no chlorine production. High salt (>5000 ppm): salty taste, possible skin feel.
Surface Impactáû High salt can be corrosive to some natural stone coping (e.g., limestone).
Equipment Impactáû Low salt can damage cell (electrolysis with insufficient conductivity ? coating degradation). High salt can trip overload/erratic reading.
Equipment' specific noteáû Check salt cell cleaning schedule (scale appears as white flaky deposits on plates).
1.8 Temperature
Roleáû Affects chemical reaction rates, chlorine demand, and swimmer comfort.
Ideal Rangeáû 25û29 ░C (78û84 ░F) for comfort. Higher temps accelerate algae growth and FC consumption.
Impact on Parametersáû For every 10 ░C rise, chemical reactions roughly double. Hot tubs require faster testing.
Equipment' specific noteáû High water temperature + high CH + high pH = rapid scaling inside heaters.

3. Chemical Adjustment Procedures
*All dosages calculated for a 40,000 L (?10,000 US gal) pool. Always verify pool volume and follow product labels. Run pump during/after addition.*
3.1 Free Chlorine (FC)
* To raise:
o Liquid chlorine (sodium hypochlorite 1012.5%): ~500 mL raises FC 1 ppm. Pour slowly in front of return jet.
o Calcium hypochlorite (granular, 6573%): pre dissolve in bucket; also adds ~7 ppm CH per 1 ppm FC.
o Dichlor (granular, 5662%): adds 0.9 ppm CYA per 1 ppm FC.
o Trichlor tablets (slow release): adds 0.6 ppm CYA per 1 ppm FC; use in feeder/skimmer only.
* To lower: Stop addition, uncover pool (sunlight). If >10 ppm, partial drain/refill.
* Safety: Gloves, goggles, ventilation.áNever mix different chlorine types, or chlorine with acid.
3.2 Combined Chlorine / Shock
* Breakpoint chlorination: raise FC to 10Î the measured CC.
Example: CC = 0.5 ppm ? add enough chlorine to increase FC by 5 ppm.
Use liquid chlorine or cal hypo. Non chlorine shock (MPS) removes CC but doesnÆt raise FC.
* Keep swimmers out until FC ?5 ppm and CC ?0.2 ppm.
3.3 pH Adjustment
* To lower:
o Muriatic acid (31.45%): ~500 mL / 40,000 L lowers pH 0.2. Pour slowly in front of return jet.
o Dry acid (sodium bisulfate): ~600 g / 40,000 L. Pre dissolve.
o Acid safety: acid resistant gloves, goggles. Always add acid to water.
* To raise:
o Soda ash (sodium carbonate): ~450 g raises pH 0.2. Pre dissolve.
o Borax: ~700 g raises pH 0.2 (gentler, also buffers).
3.4 Total Alkalinity (TA)
* To raise: Baking soda (sodium bicarbonate). ~700 g raises TA 10 ppm. Broadcast into deep end.
* To lower: Use muriatic acid in small doses (~200 mL), each addition targeting 10 ppm drop. After acid, aerate (point returns upward) to keep pH from crashing. Repeat over days.áTest daily.
3.5 Calcium Hardness (CH)
* To raise: Calcium chloride flakes (pool grade). ~1.25 kg raises CH 10 ppm. Pre dissolve, add to deep end.
* To lower: Drain and refill with soft water. Use scale inhibitor to manage existing high CH.
3.6 Cyanuric Acid (CYA)
* To raise: Stabilizer granules. ~370 g raises CYA 10 ppm. Add via skimmer sock (never directly onto surfaces). Takes 25 days to dissolve.
* To lower: Only dilution (partial drain/refill).
3.7 Salt (SWG)
* To raise: Pool salt (solar salt, 99.8% NaCl). ~18 kg raises salt ~500 ppm. Brush to dissolve; turn off cell for 24h if large addition.
* To lower: Drain and refill.
3.8 Metals & Phosphates
* Metals: Add metal sequestrant (JackÆs Magic, Metal Free) weekly. For severe staining, professional treatment needed.
* Phosphates: Lanthanum based phosphate remover (Orenda PR10,000, SeaKlear). Follow label; clean filter after treatment.
3.9 Safety & Handling Highlights
* Never mixáchlorine + acid (creates chlorine gas) or different chlorine products.
* PPE: chemical resistant gloves, goggles, mask for acids/dust.
* Add to water, not reverse; pour slowly.
* Store chemicals cool, dry, locked, separate; keep SDS sheets.
* Wait at least 46 h between incompatible chemicals; run pump.

4. Seasonal Maintenance Rhythms
4.1 Spring Opening (After Winter)
1. Clean and inspect pool, remove cover, check equipment.
2. Test fill water (well or municipal) for baseline: pH, TA, CH, metals, phosphates.
3. Balance TA first (target 80120 ppm), then pH (7.47.6).
4. Adjust CH if needed (protect plaster).
5. Add CYA to 3050 ppm (if outdoor).
6. Shock with chlorine to 1012 ppm FC; run pump 24h until FC holds and CC is 0.
7. If salt pool, add salt once water is balanced, then run SWG.
8. Clean or replace filter media (cartridge, sand, DE).
Order mattersáû TA ? pH ? CH ? CYA ? FC (shock). CYA can be added earlier because it dissolves slowly.
4.2 Peak Season (Summer, Heavy Use)
* Test FC and pH daily (or every swim day).
* Weekly full test: FC, CC, pH, TA.
* Shock after heavy bather loads or if CC >0.2.
* Monitor CYA monthly (sunlight and trichlor tablets can raise it slowly).
* For SWG pools, check salt and cell every 24 weeks; clean cell if visible scale.
* Maintain filter pressure; backwash/clean when pressure rises 810 psi.
4.3 Autumn Closing (Pre Winter)
1. Balance water: pH 7.27.6, TA 80120, CH in range.
2. Add a winter algaecide or borate treatment (optional).
3. Shock with chlorine to 1012 ppm; run pump to circulate.
4. Lower water level for closing type (mesh cover: below returns; solid cover: just below skimmer).
5. Add winter floater (trichlor) if climate allows; otherwise avoid.
6. Drain and winterize equipment (pump, filter, heater, lines).
4.4 Winter Monitoring (If not winterized)
* Test FC and pH monthly if pool is covered and not frozen.
* Run pump occasionally if water is circulating.
* Ensure salt cell is off in cold water (<1015░C).

Reality
Why It Matters
ôI smell chlorine, so I need more chlorine.ö
That smell is chloramines (combined chlorine). The pool needsáshocking, not more sanitizer.
Adding more without oxidation worsens irritation.
ôMy pH is always high; IÆll just keep adding acid.ö
High pH often caused by high TA. Lower TA first using acid + aeration method.
Dumping only acid results in pH yo yo and still high TA.
ôStabilizer (CYA) doesnÆt really matter.ö
CYA above 100 ppm locks chlorine, making sanitizer useless.
The only remedy is expensive water replacement.
ôI can use any bleach from the grocery store.ö
Only plain, unscented, nonôsplash lessö bleach (68.25%) can be used. Splash less or scented types contain additives that cause foaming and film.

ôShock and swim is fine after an hour.ö
Most shocks require FC to drop below 5 ppm; that can take 824 hours.

ôTrichlor tablets are maintenancefree.ö
Each tablet adds CYA; over a season CYA can climb to unmanageable levels.
Must monitor CYA and sometimes switch to unstabilized chlorine.
ôAlgaecide will clear my green pool.ö
Algaecide prevents, but once algae blooms, you must kill it with chlorine (shock) and brush/filter.
Using only algaecide is a losing battle.
ôI donÆt need to test calcium hardness in a vinyl pool.ö
Low CH can still corrode metal parts (ladders, pump, heater) even if no plaster.

Glossary
* Breakpoint Chlorinationáû Adding enough chlorine to oxidize all combined chlorine (usually 10Î the CC level).
* Bufferáû A chemical system (like TA) that resists change in pH.
* CalHypoáû Calcium hypochlorite; granular chlorine that also raises CH.
* Chelator / Sequestrantáû Chemical that binds metals, keeping them soluble and preventing staining.
* Chloraminesáû Combined chlorine; formed when FC combines with ammonia/organics; causes irritation and odor.
* CYA (Cyanuric Acid)áû Stabilizer that protects chlorine from UV light.
* Dichloráû Fast dissolving granular chlorine that contains CYA.
* DPDáû Reagent used in colorimetric chlorine tests (turns pink).
* FASDPDáû Titration method for FC/CC; drop counting from pink to clear; more precise than DPD.
* Free Chlorine (FC)áû The active, available chlorine that sanitizes.
* Langelier Saturation Index (LSI)áû A calculation using pH, TA, CH, temperature, and TDS to predict scale or corrosion tendency; ideally 0 ▒ 0.3.
* Muriatic Acidáû Hydrochloric acid solution, used to lower pH and TA.
* MPSáû Potassium peroxymonosulfate; non chlorine shock oxidizer.
* ORPáû Oxidation Reduction Potential; a measure of waterÆs sanitizing power (in mV).
* pHáû Measure of acidity/alkalinity; 7 neutral.
* Phosphate Removeráû Lanthanum chloride compound that precipitates phosphates.
* Salt Celláû Electrolytic cell in SWG that converts salt to chlorine.
* Scaleáû Calcium carbonate deposits from high pH + high CH.
* Shockáû Adding a large dose of oxidizer to break down contaminants and chloramines.
* Stabilizeráû See CYA.
* TA (Total Alkalinity)áû Bicarbonate content; buffer against pH changes.
* TDS (Total Dissolved Solids)áû Sum of all dissolved material; high TDS can affect water balance.
* Trichloráû Slow dissolving chlorine tablet; contains CYA.
